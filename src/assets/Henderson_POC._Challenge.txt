ü©∫ Clinical Remediation: George Henderson Case
Box 11: Principal Diagnosis Selection
Why Answer A (Cellulitis) is Wrong: Cellulitis is considered a symptom or manifestation of the underlying wound. Coding it as primary fails to capture the chronic metabolic cause (Diabetes) required for high-acuity home health reimbursement.

Why Answer C (Ischemia) is Wrong: While life-threatening, acute limb ischemia is a surgical emergency. If this were the primary reason for the visit, the patient should be in the ER, not home health.

The Correct Logic: E11.621 (Type 2 DM with foot ulcer) is the "Gold Standard" because it links the etiology to the manifestation, justifying the specialized skilled nursing needed for wound management.

Box 18: Skilled Nursing Orders (The Bone Trap)
Why Answer A (Betadine) is Wrong: Betadine is cytotoxic and can delay granulation in a diabetic wound. Furthermore, a "dry sterile dressing" is inappropriate for a Wagner Grade 3 ulcer requiring moisture balance.

Why Answer C (Soaking) is Wrong: NEVER soak a diabetic foot. Maceration of the skin increases the risk of infection spread, and hot water can cause burns due to the patient's peripheral neuropathy.

The Correct Logic: You must identify the "Probe-to-Bone" finding as suspected Osteomyelitis. This requires a specialized order (Collagen/Foam) and immediate MD notification for advanced imaging (X-ray) and potential surgical debridement.

Box 21: Frequency (The Schedule Trap)
Why Answer B (7w1) is Wrong: Although the note mentions "daily oversight," it specifies this is only for the first 72 hours. In CMS-485 coding, this is represented as 3 visits in week 1 (3w1), not a full week of daily visits.

Why Answer D (PT 1w1) is Wrong: Adding a "1w1" evaluation for PT is a common mistake; however, the narrative states PT will see the patient twice a week for three weeks after a 48-hour hold. This is documented as 2w3, not as a separate evaluation week.

The Correct Logic: SN: 3w1, 2w3, 1w4 matches the narrative's specific timeline of 72 hours stabilization, 3 remaining weeks of the first month, and the 4 weeks of the second month.

Critical Priority: The "911 Moment"
The Remediation: If the learner prioritized the wound over the heart rate/firearm, they failed the "Safety First" test.

Clinical Rationale:

Cardiac: A heart rate of 48 (Bradycardia) in an ashen, diaphoretic diabetic patient is a Silent MI until proven otherwise.

Vascular: The cold, pulseless L leg indicates an acute blockage that can lead to amputation within hours.

Safety: You cannot perform clinical care in the presence of an unsecured firearm and a confused patient. Safety and life-stabilization are always the first "Skilled Actions".
